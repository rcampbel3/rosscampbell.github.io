The Great Halloween Skeleton Conspiracy

Every October, a curious phenomenon sweeps through our stores and front yards: creatures that never had bones suddenly sport complete skeletal systems. Animals with exoskeletons get internal ribcages. Soft-bodied invertebrates mysteriously develop vertebrae. It's as if some mad scientist decided that everything deserves to look like a tiny, creepy human.

But why? The answer is delightfully simple: We're anthropomorphizing death itself. Humans are so fond of our own skeletal structure that we can't resist projecting it onto every living thing, regardless of how anatomically absurd the result may be.

üß† The Psychology Behind the Bones
We recognize things by their bones better than their soft tissues. When you strip away fur, feathers, and flesh, animals become harder to identify. So decoration designers add bone ears, exaggerated skulls, and human-like features to make sure we know we're looking at a "dog" or a "cat"‚Äîeven though real dog and cat skeletons look remarkably different from their plastic counterparts. It's instant recognizability over anatomical accuracy!
